The Labyrinth


You jump into the hole, heart thudding like a drum in your chest. The silence swallows you instantly. No wind, no echo, no voice now, just the cold rush of nothing pulling you down. You are scared. Of the dark, of falling, of being wrong. But the voice said this would work. It told you this was the only way to stop the drought, to save the fields, the rivers, your home. You want to believe it. You need to believe it. So you close your eyes and fall, clutching that last shred of hope like a secret.


. . .

When you open your eyes again, you are lying on the cobblestone path just outside the cottage. The sky is tinted orange with late afternoon light. Your head aches. Your clothes are dusty and your limbs feel heavy. You push yourself up and stumble toward the porch. Your stepmother is there, and turns around to hang up a piece of laundry. When she sees you, her arms cross, and her mouth tightens with anger.

Where are the berries?

You blink at her, trying to steady your voice. "I fell in a hole into this weird labyrinth. There was a voice. It said I could stop the drought." Your stepmother stares at you, unmoved. Then she laughs, but there is no joy in it.

You expect me to believe that? Voices? Holes? Do you think I am stupid?

You shake your head. "I'm not lying. I wanted to help our forest. I really did. I thought I could stop it. I thought I was close... I was lied to ma..."

You're hearing voices now. That's all the information I need to send you away.

You feel the tears start before you can stop them. "I tried," you whisper. "I almost did it. I just wanted to protect the flora... please...mom..." You see her expression harden.

I'm glad I finally have an excuse. I've had enough of your stories and your strangeness.

By nightfall, you are in the town's asylum. The walls are white. The bed is too clean. The door closes with a soft final sound, and no one looks at you as the lock clicks into place.

My mom didn't even say goodbye to me.
